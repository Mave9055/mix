How to Use This Script
This document is a word-for-word facilitator script for the 70-minute thirteenth session of What Really Happened. Deliver it as written until the language is yours. The goal is to sound like yourself saying these words — not like someone reading a document.
SCRIPT KEY
	Regular text = speak this aloud
	

	[Stage directions] = action, pause, or movement cue — do not read aloud
	

	FACILITATOR NOTE = read before the session, not during
	

	WATCH FOR = warning signs to monitor in the room
	

	DEEP DIVE = optional expansion if time and group readiness allow
	Timing targets are included for each section. Session 13 is 70 minutes total. This is the first session of Part V — the turn outward. The shift in register matters: Sessions 1 through 12 were about what happened and what it built. Sessions 13 through 16 are about what to do with it. Hold that shift from the opening.

Pre-Session Facilitator Notes
FACILITATOR NOTE
	This session makes a claim that sounds like an insult and isn't: you are unskilled in specific, nameable ways — not because of any flaw in character, but because the conditions that teach those skills were not present. The distinction between broken and unskilled is the clinical and ethical spine of the session. Hold it clearly. Broken leads nowhere. Unskilled leads to a late curriculum, which is uncomfortable and possible.
	

	The session opens in Part V — Changing the World Around You. The register shift matters. Sessions 1 through 12 mapped the wound and its mechanisms. Session 13 begins the turn toward what can be done with that knowledge. The audit framing — naming the gap accurately so it can be addressed — is the through-line. Keep returning to it when the discussion drifts toward shame.
	

	The regulation tax material is the most practically useful content in this session for many participants. Many will have been punished, fired, labeled, or abandoned over symptoms — lateness, inability to initiate, frustration tolerance evaporating — that have never been named as downstream effects of the threat-management overhead. Name that cost explicitly. It has been invisible.
	

	The peace paradox close — the quiet Tuesday the nervous system is currently scanning for threat — needs to be delivered as a live demonstration, not as a concept. The closing grounding is designed to put participants inside the experience of the paradox in real time. Trust the design.
	

WATCH FOR
	Participants who laugh with recognition at the glass wall description — the feeling of watching other people function from the other side of an invisible barrier. That laugh is almost always relief: finally a name for something that has felt like a personal failing for decades. Receive it.
	

	Anyone who becomes visibly activated — not sad, activated — during the regulation tax material. They may be placing specific recent consequences — job loss, relationship failure, practical life consequences — onto the mechanism in real time. Give it space.
	

	The participant who goes very still at peace boring, sign healing — not wrong. That image lands differently for people who have been in the peace paradox long enough to have started chasing chaos deliberately, or who have ended relationships or situations specifically because the quiet felt wrong. Let the stillness be.
	

	Participants who resist the late curriculum framing — who hear unskilled as an insult despite the framing. That resistance is worth noting privately. The shame response to unskilled is itself a demonstration of the gap the session is naming. Don't challenge it in the group.
	

	Anyone who tears up during the self-compassion passage — the same framework extended to yourself that you readily extend to others. This is often the first time participants have heard that the extension of compassion to themselves is not weakness but the same mechanism they already use for everyone else.

Part 1: Opening Grounding
⏱ 5 minutes — Do not cut this short
[Same posture. This session opens Part V and carries a different register than the sessions before it. The grounding should feel steady and forward-facing rather than bracing.]
Feet on the floor.
[Pause 5 seconds.]
Weight in the chair.
[Pause 5 seconds.]
Three things that aren't a threat.
[Pause 8 seconds.]
I want to say something before we begin, because this session is going to ask something of you that is different from what the last twelve sessions asked.
Sessions 1 through 12 looked backward and inward. They traced what happened, what it built, what it costs, what it looks like from inside a nervous system that learned everything it knows in conditions that were never supposed to be the baseline.
This session turns the direction. Not away from any of that — it's all still true and it all still matters. But toward something more specific: what wasn't taught, and what can still be learned.
The session is going to tell you that you are unskilled in specific ways. I want to be clear before we go there that unskilled and broken are not the same sentence. Broken leads nowhere. Unskilled leads to a curriculum — late, uncomfortable, and possible.
One slow breath in.
[Breathe audibly. Model it.]
And out.
[Pause 5 seconds.]
We're starting at the glass wall.

Part 2: The Lecture — The Lessons Nobody Taught Us
⏱ 25 minutes
________________


2A. The Glass Wall
⏱ 8 minutes
He watched them from the other side of the glass.
Kids at school who seemed carefree — obsessed with things that felt insignificant from where he stood. Adults who handled minor disagreements and moved on. People who navigated a grocery store, paid a bill, scheduled a repair, returned a call — and none of it appeared to cost them anything.
He couldn't locate the mechanism.
The tasks that seemed to require no cognitive overhead for everyone else required, from him, something closer to preparation for combat.
A deep, gut-level sense of being separate. Looking at life through a glass wall. Even at work, surrounded by people, feeling utterly alone in my internal world.
[Pause 4 seconds.]
The glass wall isn't metaphor. It is the NICU made permanent — the thirty days of acrylic and alarms from Session 1 that taught the nervous system to experience proximity as something that happens on the other side of a barrier. By the time he was old enough to be in rooms with other people, the barrier was internal and invisible and total. He was present. He was watching. He was not inside the same reality the other people appeared to inhabit.
[Pause 3 seconds.]
What he was watching them do — the thing that looked effortless from the outside — was execute skills he had never been taught.
Not because he was stupid. Not because he was broken. Because the conditions that teach those skills are specific, and those conditions had not been present.
[Pause 3 seconds.]
The skills that get taught in safe, regulated childhoods are not dramatic. They are ordinary. Naming what you feel before it becomes a crisis. Setting a limit without it feeling like an attack or an abandonment. Tolerating discomfort without the nervous system treating it as an emergency. Sitting in a quiet room without the quiet reading as threat.
These are not innate capacities. They are learned — through thousands of micro-interactions with regulated caregivers who model them, name them, and repair the ruptures when they fail. That curriculum requires safety and consistency. Neither was available.
So instead of that curriculum, the available one ran: hide the feeling before it causes conflict. Disappear before you're made to disappear. When you don't know what to do, keep moving and hope the motion is enough.
That curriculum produced its own competencies — extraordinary ones, in some domains. The hypervigilance that reads a room in three seconds. The capacity to function in crisis when everyone else freezes. The endurance that runs on emergency power for years.
What it didn't produce was the ability to function on a quiet Tuesday.
________________


2B. The Regulation Tax
⏱ 9 minutes
I was constantly late — ten minutes, fifteen, sometimes thirty. Not because I didn't care, but because there was a war in my chest before every shift.
A war in the chest before every shift.
[Pause 4 seconds.]
Not a preference for lateness. Not disrespect for other people's time. Not a character flaw that could be corrected by caring more or trying harder. A nervous system that treats the transition from one state to another — from home to work, from alone to public, from unstructured to structured — as a threat requiring preparation.
The alarm fires on the ordinary. The executive function goes offline before the day has started. The cognitive budget is spent on regulation before a single task has been attempted.
[Pause 3 seconds.]
This is what I want to name as the regulation tax — because it is the most practically significant and least-named mechanism in the daily experience of CPTSD:
The cognitive and emotional overhead required to maintain baseline regulation before any functional task begins. In a nervous system calibrated to chronic threat, that overhead is very high. What remains for sequencing, planning, initiating, following through — the machinery of ordinary adult function — is what's left after the tax has been paid.
Some days the tax takes most of the budget. The boxes stay unpacked. The calls go unreturned. The forms don't get filed. From the outside, that looks like avoidance, like dysfunction, like a person who doesn't care about their own life. From the inside, it is a system that has already spent its available resources on staying regulated enough to be in the room at all.
The mental clutter, the unpacked boxes, the frustration tolerance of a gnat sometimes.
The frustration tolerance that evaporates at the precise moment it's most needed. That's not a personality trait. That's a system running near capacity — where one additional demand tips the remaining regulation reserve into deficit. The frustration isn't disproportionate to the situation. It's proportionate to everything that situation is asking of a system that is already running in the red.
[Pause 4 seconds.]
I want to say something that this course does not say enough: the people who were punished for these symptoms — fired for the lateness, abandoned for the unpacked boxes, labeled for the frustration — were not being punished for character flaws. They were being punished for the downstream effects of a threat-management system that was never designed for the conditions it was being asked to run in.
That does not erase the impact of the lateness or the boxes or the frustration on the people who experienced them. It locates where the behavior came from. Session 9 gave us the frame for holding both. This session is applying it to the ordinary.
DEEP DIVE
	For clinically trained groups: What we're describing maps to the research on allostatic load — the cumulative cost of chronic stress on the body's regulatory systems. McEwen's work consistently demonstrates that sustained allostatic load depletes the prefrontal cortical resources available for executive function, impulse regulation, and cognitive flexibility. The clinical implication is direct: interventions that address only the behavioral output — the lateness, the avoidance, the frustration — without addressing the allostatic load that is consuming the regulatory budget will produce limited and unsustainable results. The regulation tax has to be reduced before the behaviors it produces can reliably change.
	

	For general groups: skip the citation. The plain version — the tax has to be paid before any other task begins, and on high-overhead days the tax takes most of what's available — is sufficient.

2C. The Peace Paradox and the Late Curriculum
⏱ 8 minutes
There is one more mechanism this session needs to name, because it is the one that most directly sabotages the healing that the previous twelve sessions have been building toward.
For a nervous system calibrated to chronic threat, calm is not neutral. Calm is a signal.
When you live like that, your brain changes. It stops looking for peace and starts scanning for threats. Tragedy is the only thing that feels real. Relaxation feels foreign, even unsafe, because the body is always braced for the next threat.
The quiet breaks the pattern. And when the alarm doesn't fire — when nothing is wrong, when the Tuesday is genuinely ordinary — the nervous system reads the quiet as the eye of the hurricane. The precursor to the next strike. The calm before the thing that always comes after the calm.
[Pause 4 seconds.]
Peace boring, sign healing — not wrong.
That line is the manual entry that nobody wrote at seven. Or seventeen. Or thirty-five. The boredom of an unalarmed Tuesday is not emptiness. It is the window of tolerance doing exactly what it is supposed to do — holding a state that is neither flooded nor frozen, just present, just here, just ordinary.
That state is the goal. It feels like nothing because what it is is the absence of emergency. An entire life spent in emergency makes the absence of it feel like deprivation. And so the nervous system reaches for the familiar frequency — creates urgency, accelerates the pace, finds something wrong, finds someone who will produce the correct kind of chaos — because the familiar frequency feels like aliveness, and the window of tolerance feels like waiting to die.
[Pause 3 seconds.]
The vocabulary arrived late for all of it.
Complex Trauma. Attachment Theory. Nervous System Dysregulation. Window of Tolerance. DBT. Grounding. Self-compassion. Each term arriving as translation for something that had been running in the body for decades without a name. And with the name — not a cure, but a change in what was possible. What you can name, you can begin to address specifically rather than continuing to treat its symptoms as evidence of who you are.
Learning self-compassion was like finally offering myself the same kindness and understanding I so readily gave to others. It was a revolutionary act.
[Pause 4 seconds.]
Revolutionary. Not helpful. Not therapeutic. Revolutionary — because extending to yourself the same framework you extend to others runs directly against the operating logic of a system that learned at six years old that its own presence was conditional and its own pain was evidence of fault.
The late curriculum is uncomfortable. Being a beginner at something most people learned before they had language is uncomfortable. It requires the same patience that would be extended to anyone learning something for the first time — and that patience is exactly the thing the nervous system was never taught to extend to itself.
That is the audit this session is conducting. Not to produce shame about the gap. To name the gap accurately enough that it can be addressed — specifically, deliberately, one small skill at a time.

Part 3: Group Discussion
⏱ 20 minutes
FACILITATOR NOTE
	This discussion is asking participants to name specific unnamed skills — not general patterns, not dramatic failures, but specific ordinary things that require more than they should. Keep the conversation close to the concrete. The risk in this discussion is that it drifts toward either shame or abstraction. Redirect toward the specific: "What is the exact task? What does the alarm cost you before you've even started it?"
	

	The question you're holding throughout: not what is wrong with you, but what specifically wasn't taught, and what is teaching it now — even imperfectly, even late.
	Discussion Prompt 1
He watched other people function from the other side of the glass wall. What specific ordinary task — not a crisis, not a major life event, just a Tuesday task — has consistently required from you what appears to cost other people nothing? What do you think you are doing when they are running on automatic?
Discussion Prompt 2
The war in the chest before every shift. The nervous system treating transition as threat. Think about your own transitions — morning to work, private to public, unstructured to structured. What does the alarm cost you before you've even begun the task? What would it mean to name that cost accurately rather than treating it as a character flaw?
Discussion Prompt 3
Peace boring, sign healing — not wrong. Think of a specific quiet Tuesday — genuinely unalarmed, nothing wrong. What did your nervous system do with that quiet? Did it read as rest or as threat? What did you do in response?
Discussion Prompt 4
The late curriculum: skills that should have been taught in childhood being learned for the first time in adulthood. What is one specific skill — naming a feeling, setting a limit, tolerating discomfort without treating it as emergency — that you are learning now, as an adult, for what feels like the first time? What is it like to be a beginner at something that others appear to do automatically?
Discussion Prompt 5
Learning self-compassion was a revolutionary act. The same framework extended to yourself that you readily extend to others. Where is the gap widest for you — between how you explain other people's behavior and how you explain your own equivalent behavior? What would close that gap even slightly?

Part 4: Journal / Worksheet
⏱ 10 minutes — Individual, quiet
Distribute the Session 13 worksheet. Allow participants to write in silence.
FACILITATOR NOTE
	Before participants begin, say the following: "This prompt asks for something small and specific — not a major life skill, not a dramatic failure, but one specific small lesson that nobody taught you. How to sit still without scanning. How to let a quiet morning be quiet. Something ordinary that should have been easy and wasn't. That specificity is the point. The small and specific is where the late curriculum actually runs." Then give them the full ten minutes.
	Worksheet Prompts:
* Name the lesson nobody taught you that you most needed — something small and specific, not a major life skill. How to sit still without scanning. How to let a quiet morning be quiet. How to feel frustrated without the frustration becoming an emergency. How to start a task without first defeating the war in your chest.
* Where should that lesson have been taught? Who should have taught it? What were they doing instead?
* Who or what is teaching it to you now — even imperfectly, even late?
________________


Part 5: Closing Grounding
⏱ 10 minutes
[Full pause after the writing. This session has been asking participants to look at specific ordinary gaps — things they've been compensating for without naming for years. Give the room time to settle.]
Feet on the floor.
[Wait 5 seconds.]
Weight in the chair.
[Wait 5 seconds.]
Three things that aren't a threat.
[Wait 8 seconds.]
One breath in.
[Breathe with them.]
And out.
[Wait 5 seconds.]
I want to close with something that is both a fact and a demonstration.
You are not broken. You are also unskilled in specific ways — because the conditions that teach those skills were not present. Those are different statements. Only the second one leads somewhere.
[Pause 4 seconds.]
The regulation tax is real. The war in the chest before every shift is real. The glass wall is real. Naming them accurately is not self-pity. It is the prerequisite for addressing them specifically rather than continuing to treat their symptoms as character flaws.
[Pause 4 seconds.]
Now notice this: right now, this is a quiet moment. Nothing is required of you. The session is almost over. There is no task, no transition, no demand pending.
Your nervous system may be scanning it for what comes next. Let it scan.
[Pause 6 seconds.]
Notice: nothing came. The scan found nothing. The quiet is just quiet.
[Pause 4 seconds.]
That is the lesson. You are in it right now — the window of tolerance, functional, present, a nervous system that fired the scan and settled. That is the late curriculum in practice. That is what boring feels like when boring is working.
Session 14 is about what happens when you take everything this course has taught you and turn it toward the systems that failed you — the courts, the rehab centers, the institutions that were supposed to help and made things worse. We'll see you then.
[Remain available. The regulation tax and late curriculum material sometimes surfaces acute shame about specific practical failures — job losses, relationship endings, practical consequences — that participants have been carrying as character evidence for years. Expect private conversations. Do not rush out.]

Appendix: Clinical Reference Notes for Facilitator
Not for distribution. Facilitator preparation only.
Key Terms Introduced in Session 13
Term
	Plain-Language Definition
	Regulation tax
	The cognitive and emotional overhead required to maintain baseline regulation before any functional task begins. In a nervous system calibrated to chronic threat, this overhead is very high. What remains for ordinary adult function is what's left after the tax has been paid.
	The glass wall
	The nervous system's experience of proximity as something that happens on the other side of a barrier — present in the room, watching from outside the reality others appear to share. Not chosen. Not curable by effort. Addressable through repeated experiences of genuine safety.
	Transition as threat
	The nervous system treating the movement from one state to another — home to public, alone to social, unstructured to structured — as a threat requiring preparation. The alarm firing on the ordinary. The cognitive budget spent on the transition before the destination has been reached.
	The peace paradox
	For a nervous system calibrated to chronic threat, calm reads as the eye of the hurricane rather than a state of safety. Boredom on an unalarmed Tuesday is not emptiness — it is the window of tolerance functioning correctly. It feels like deprivation because it is the absence of emergency.
	Late curriculum
	The skills that should have been taught through thousands of micro-interactions with regulated caregivers — naming feelings, tolerating discomfort, setting limits, repairing ruptures — being learned for the first time in adulthood. Not shameful. The normal result of a childhood that couldn't provide what the curriculum required.
	Trauma literacy as translation
	Clinical vocabulary arriving as translation for processes that have been running in the body for decades without names. The name doesn't change the mechanism. It changes what can be done with the mechanism.
	What Session 13 Accomplishes Clinically
* Opens Part V by turning the course's lens outward — from what happened and what it built, toward what can be done with that knowledge
* Introduces the regulation tax as a named mechanism for the daily practical consequences of CPTSD — which addresses one of the most commonly punished and least-named presentations
* Names the glass wall as the NICU made permanent — connecting the developmental origin directly to the adult experience of isolation inside proximity
* Introduces transition as threat as the specific mechanism behind lateness and executive dysfunction — which removes the moral framing from behaviors that have been consistently punished as character flaws
* Names the peace paradox explicitly — which gives participants language for one of the most self-sabotaging and least-understood CPTSD presentations
* Introduces the late curriculum as a frame for adult skill acquisition — which positions the discomfort of being a beginner as normal and expected rather than evidence of irreparable deficit
* Demonstrates the peace paradox in the closing grounding — putting participants inside the experience of the quiet Tuesday and naming it as the window of tolerance functioning correctly
* Sets up Session 14 by establishing that understanding the gap between what was taught and what should have been taught leads directly to the question of systemic accountability — who was responsible for providing those conditions and didn't
