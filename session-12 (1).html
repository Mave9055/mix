Session 12  ·  Safety Protocol

Crisis, Suicide Risk, and Staying Alive

Part IV — The Internal Battlefield

70 Minutes Safety Protocol High Activation Risk Resources Required
What This Session Does

This session does not treat suicide risk as a topic to be introduced carefully and then moved past. It treats it as a nervous system event — something that happens inside a body under specific conditions, not a character flaw or a decision made from strength or weakness.

The case study for this session is the most direct in the curriculum. During COVID, the author had a noose in his closet. He knew it was there. He didn't use it — not because of a hotline, not because of a plan, not because of an intervention. He didn't use it because he was afraid of the pain before dying. That's it. That's what held.

This session teaches participants what was actually happening in that closet — and in every version of that closet that the people in this room have stood near. It names the nervous system conditions that produce suicidal ideation. It makes a distinction between wanting to die and needing the pain to stop. And it teaches what actually helps, not what looks good in a pamphlet.

The goal is not to scare the room. The goal is to make the room safe enough that someone who is currently in this material can say so — and know what to do next.

Key Concept

Most people who reach the edge are not trying to die. They are trying to stop a pain that feels permanent and inescapable. The intervention isn't heroics — it's creating enough doubt about the permanence. One crack of light is enough. The nervous system will take it.

Key Terms
Vocabulary for This Session
Suicidal Ideation
Thoughts about ending one's life. Exists on a spectrum from passive ("I wish I wasn't here") to active (specific plans and means). Ideation is a nervous system signal, not a moral failure. Its presence means the pain load has exceeded the nervous system's current capacity to manage it — not that the person is broken or dangerous.
Passive vs. Active Ideation
Passive ideation is the wish to not exist — without a plan, without a method. Active ideation involves a specific plan and/or access to means. Both require attention. The difference matters for response, not for whether the person deserves to be taken seriously.
Means and Access
The method and its availability. The single most evidence-based intervention in suicide prevention is means restriction — creating distance between a person in crisis and the specific method they're considering. Distance buys time. Time allows the nervous system to regulate. Most crises pass.
Abandonment Terror
A specific nervous system state triggered by the loss or threatened loss of primary attachment. In people with complex trauma — who were already wired for hypervigilance around abandonment — the departure of a family, a partner, or children can hit the nervous system with the same force as a physical threat. This is not metaphor. The brain processes social rejection in the same regions as physical pain.
Pressure Cooker State
When multiple major stressors compound simultaneously — grief, job stress, relationship collapse, substance use, isolation — the nervous system's capacity to regulate is overwhelmed from multiple directions at once. No single stressor caused the crisis. The system simply ran out of room.
Regulatory Attachment
Using connection to another person as a nervous system regulation strategy. This is not weakness — it is how human nervous systems are designed to work. The complication arises when the attachment is itself unstable or when the regulation depends on a single person. What works in the short term (connection that turns down the volume) can become a trauma bond when the underlying pain is never addressed.
Pre-Session
Facilitator Notes

Have resources physically available before this session begins. Not on a slide. Not on a handout at the end. On the table, visible, at the start.

This session comes directly after Session 07.5 — the work-as-dissociation session — for a reason. That session surfaces things people have been running from. This session is what happens when the running stops and the weight is still there.

You are not a clinician. You are not expected to assess risk or make clinical decisions. Your job in this session is to hold the room, name what you're seeing, and know when to stop the session and get someone more help. That is a complete job. It is not a failure if this session needs to pause for a person in real time.

Do not rush the case study. The specificity is the point. A facilitator reading a statistic will not reach the person who has been where this case study goes. The details are the intervention.

⚠ Watch For

Participants who go very quiet and still during the case study. That stillness is not disengagement. Check in at the break, not in front of the group.

Participants who laugh or deflect. Same as Session 07.5 — that laugh is recognition. Let it land and then stay with the material.

Participants who disclose active ideation during discussion. Stop the discussion. Address it directly. Do not manage it quietly at the side of the room while the session continues. The group already knows something happened. Name it plainly: "We're going to pause here because what [name] just shared matters more than the schedule."

Anyone who connects this session to a current situation, not a past one. Past tense is processing. Present tense is crisis. The difference in a single word changes your response entirely.

Opening Grounding · 5 min

"Before we start today — feet on the floor. Weight in the chair. One breath.

Today's session goes somewhere real. I'm not going to pretend otherwise. We're going to talk about crisis, about suicidal thinking, about what it actually feels like from the inside — not from a textbook.

If at any point today you need to step out, step out. If you need to talk to me privately, tell me. If something I say lands in a place that's current for you — not past, current — please let me know. You don't have to announce it to the room. You just have to let me know.

The resources on the table are there before we start because they belong at the beginning, not the end. You don't have to be in crisis to use them. You don't have to have a plan. You just have to be carrying something that's gotten too heavy.

That's enough."

Lecture · 30 min
The Case Study + Clinical Content
Speak slowly. Let each section land before you move. This is not a fast session.

The Setup

It's 2020. COVID has shut everything down. The author is managing a restaurant — the governor has closed everything except takeout, so it's just him and one other cook. Everyone else he had to lay off.

He had people begging him to keep them on. People with kids to feed. People he knew. He had to tell all of them no. He carried that home every night.

At home, his marriage was ending. Not in the clean, acknowledged way — in the slow, grinding way where everyone knows and nobody says it. His wife had signed a lease on a new place. His name wasn't on it. She had told the kids they didn't have to listen to him anymore. He'd get home from work and they would chant it at him: we don't have to do what you say. We don't have to do what you say.

So he'd go straight to his room. Stay there until work the next morning. That was home.

He relapsed.

The Pressure Cooker

This is what the nervous system was carrying simultaneously:

  • Grief over the job losses — people he was responsible for
  • The knowledge that his family was leaving — signed lease, no name on it
  • Daily humiliation in the place that was supposed to be home
  • Isolation — a room until morning, every night
  • Relapse — which in a C-PTSD nervous system doesn't just mean using again, it means every story you've ever told yourself about being broken just got confirmed
  • A pandemic — the entire external world locked down, no exits, no relief

No single one of those things breaks a person. All of them at once, with no way out and no room to breathe — that's a pressure cooker. That's what produces the closet.

The Closet

There was a noose in the closet. He knew it was there. He didn't use it.

— pause —

If you ask him what stopped him, he'll tell you: fear of the pain before dying.

Not a hotline. Not a reason to live. Not a moment of clarity. Fear of the pain before dying.

I want you to hear that without judgment — because that answer tells you everything about what suicidal crisis actually is and what actually intervenes in it.

He wasn't trying to die. He was trying to stop a pain that felt like it had no floor, no ceiling, no exit. The noose wasn't a destination. It was the only door he could see in a room that had stopped having walls.

The intervention wasn't heroic. It was a crack of light — one physical doubt about what dying would feel like — and the nervous system took it.

That's how close it is for most people. Not dramatic. Not announced. One small thing that creates enough hesitation to survive the night.

What the Nervous System Is Actually Doing

Suicidal ideation is not a moral failure. It is not weakness. It is a nervous system that has exceeded its regulatory capacity and is generating the most extreme solution available to end the threat.

The nervous system doesn't know the difference between a threat you can walk away from and a threat that will follow you. It just knows the pain load is too high and something has to give.

In people with complex trauma — people who were wired early for hypervigilance, for freeze, for anticipating the next hit — that threshold is lower. Not because they're weaker. Because the system has been running hot for years. It doesn't take as much to push a nervous system over the edge when that nervous system has never fully come off high alert.

And relapse — especially in that context — doesn't just mean using. It means the shame response fires at full volume. Every story the person has been carrying about being broken, being a burden, being unfixable — all of it confirms itself. The nervous system is now managing the crisis and the shame of the crisis at the same time.

That's the pressure cooker state. Multiple systems overwhelmed simultaneously. And it rarely announces itself. It usually looks, from the outside, like someone going quiet.

When the Family Left

The crisis didn't end when the pressure cooker peaked. It shifted.

When his family actually left — not the slow knowing, but the actual departure — the suicidal thinking came back at a different volume. He described it this way:

"It was like being a five-year-old dumped off at the mountains and told to find your way back to town."

His words. Exactly as he said them.

That's not metaphor for sadness. That's the clinical definition of abandonment terror — a nervous system that was already wired for hypervigilance around abandonment, re-experiencing the original wound at full force through an adult loss.

The brain processes social rejection in the same neural pathways as physical pain. The five-year-old lost in the mountains is not a figure of speech. That is the actual experience — the size, the helplessness, the not-knowing-how-to-get-back — running through an adult body with no map and no one to call.

That's what the people in this room have felt. Maybe not that specific loss. But that specific size. That specific alone.

— pause —

What pulled him back from that second crisis wasn't a plan. It was a person. A woman named Tina — someone to talk to, someone to be near. When he was with her the suicidal thinking dropped. So he stayed near her as much as he could.

He knows now that was a trauma bond. His nervous system found regulation through attachment because it had no other tools. That's not a character flaw. That's a nervous system doing exactly what human nervous systems are built to do — seeking co-regulation when internal regulation has failed.

The complication isn't that he sought connection. The complication is that connection without healing is a temporary fix that defers the underlying pain — the same mechanism as work in Session 07.5, just with a person instead of a job.

What he needed — what everyone in a moment like that needs — is for someone to help make the pain feel less permanent. That's the intervention. Not heroics. Not a speech. Just enough presence to make the room feel survivable for one more night.

What Actually Helps

The research on suicide intervention is consistent on one point that most people don't know: means restriction is the single most effective intervention. Creating distance between a person in crisis and the specific method they're considering. Not removing the desire — removing the immediate access. Distance buys time. Time allows the nervous system to regulate. The majority of people who survive a suicidal crisis do not go on to die by suicide. The crisis passes. The pain that felt permanent turns out to have a floor.

What also helps, consistently: connection. Not advice. Not solutions. Not talking them out of it. Presence. Someone in the room. Someone who doesn't flinch. Someone who makes the night feel survivable long enough to get to morning.

What doesn't help: telling someone they have so much to live for. Telling them other people have it worse. Telling them it's a permanent solution to a temporary problem. These are all true. They land as dismissal. They confirm what the nervous system already believes — that the pain is too inconvenient to hold, that someone needs to fix it quickly so the other person can move on.

Sound familiar? That's the same mechanism as Session 07.5. Shut up. I have work tomorrow.

Group Discussion · 20 min
Discussion Prompts
Go slow. Do not rush from one prompt to the next. If the room needs to stay on the first question for the full 20 minutes, stay there. The prompts exist to open doors, not to be completed.

  1. Have you ever been in a pressure cooker state — where multiple things collapsed at the same time and the weight stopped feeling manageable? You don't have to name everything in it. Just: what did that feel like in your body?

  2. The session makes a distinction between wanting to die and wanting the pain to stop. Does that distinction land for you? Does it change how you think about moments you've had — or moments you've seen in people you know?

  3. The case study describes connection — being near Tina — as what turned the volume down. Has there been a person, a place, or a thing that did that for you in a crisis moment? What was it about that specific thing that made the room feel survivable?

  4. What does it feel like to hear that fear of the pain before dying is a valid thing that kept someone here? Does that change anything about how you think about your own survival?

Individual Reflection · 10 min
Journal Prompt
Write

Write about a time when the pain felt like it had no floor. Not necessarily the worst moment — the moment that feels most true to what this session described. What was the pressure cooker? What was in it? And what — even something small, even something you're embarrassed to name — created enough light to survive the night?

If this session is current for you rather than past — if what you're writing about is happening now, not then — please tell the facilitator before you leave this room. That's not weakness. That's the bravest thing you can do today.

Closing Grounding · 5 min

"Before you leave — feet on the floor. Hand on your chest if you want it. Feel your heartbeat.

You stayed in the room today. That's not nothing. This material is heavy and you stayed.

If something from today is still sitting in you when you walk out — if the pressure cooker description felt too familiar, if the mountain line hit somewhere specific — please don't take that home alone. Use the numbers on the table. Text a person you trust. Tell someone what's sitting in you.

You don't have to have a plan to deserve support. You don't have to be in crisis. If the weight got heavier in this room today, that's enough reason to reach out.

You survived whatever brought you here. That survival is real. It counts.

Take a breath. You can go."

Clinical Appendix

Van der Kolk, The Body Keeps the Score (2014): On the neurological basis of suicidal ideation in complex trauma; the role of hyperarousal and shutdown in crisis states.

Joiner, Why People Die by Suicide (2005): The interpersonal theory of suicide — thwarted belonging and perceived burdensomeness as the two primary drivers of suicidal ideation in adults. Both are directly present in the case study.

Means restriction research: Harvard T.H. Chan School of Public Health — consistent evidence that creating distance between a person in crisis and their intended method is the single most effective structural intervention. Most crises are time-limited. Distance buys the time required.

Porges, Polyvagal Theory: On co-regulation as a primary nervous system function — why connection with another regulated nervous system is not a weakness but the biological mechanism by which crisis states resolve.

Connection to Case Study: The Tina sequence described in Chapter 8 is a clinically clean example of regulatory attachment in crisis — the nervous system seeking co-regulation through proximity when internal regulation has failed. The subsequent recognition of it as a trauma bond does not invalidate its function in the acute phase. It worked. The complication is long-term reliance without underlying processing.

Note on Corrections Populations: In carceral settings, suicidal ideation presents differently — often as talk of "checking out," indifference to consequences, deliberate rule violations that invite punishment, or requests for solitary. Direct questions are appropriate and do not increase risk. Asking someone if they are thinking about suicide does not plant the idea. It creates permission to tell the truth.

Note on Session Sequencing: This session sits downstream of Session 07.5 deliberately. The work-as-dissociation session surfaces grief that has been deferred. This session is the container for what surfaces. Facilitators should treat the two sessions as a single two-part intervention when possible.
