The Case Study

Why a Case Study

The argument against abstraction. Why one specific life teaches more than ten aggregate descriptions.

The Methodology

The Power of Specificity

Complex PTSD is often taught through definitions, symptom lists, and diagnostic criteria. These are accurate but incomplete. They describe the shape of the thing without conveying what it feels like to live inside it.

A case study changes that. It moves from abstraction to lived experience. It shows not just what hypervigilance is, but what it looks like when a body circles an entire building to find the back entrance. Not what dissociation is, but what it feels like when the thinking brain disconnects and the body keeps moving through a day it cannot remember.

The curriculum uses one documented life as the primary teaching material. Every clinical concept is grounded in a specific event, relationship, or moment from that life. This is not anecdotal — it is methodological. Specificity is the mechanism by which abstraction becomes usable.

"A concept you can only hold in your head is a concept you cannot use in your body."

The Source Material

From the Storm to the Fire

The curriculum is built from From the Storm to the Fire, a published memoir that documents the full arc of this nervous system's journey. The memoir is the primary source material for every session. It is not optional reading — it is the foundation.

"Three pounds, four ounces of undercooked soul dropped into a box of glass and wires. A soul too new already prepared for departure. Then something shifted — and the vitals started rising."

The memoir is available through standard book distribution channels. It provides the context, the specificity, and the human voice that makes the curriculum clinically usable. Facilitators are encouraged to read it before running the sessions. Participants often read it after completing the curriculum.

The Thread

The Decision to Stay

The nervous system that chose to keep going before it had language for choosing. This is the thread that runs across all 18 sessions. Not triumph. Not inspiration. Not a recovery narrative that resolves neatly. But the specific, documented moment-to-moment choice to stay alive when staying alive made no logical sense.

The curriculum teaches from this thread. It does not soften it. It does not make it inspirational. It names it as what it is: the mechanism by which a nervous system that was built for survival learned to survive its own survival.

This is what makes the curriculum different. It is not built on the story of recovery. It is built on the story of staying. And for the people who have circled buildings to avoid the front entrance, who have made decisions their thinking brain did not understand, who have survived systems that were designed to fail them — that distinction changes everything.
